The Four-Part Prompt Framework

Most people use AI at about 10% of its potential because they make one critical mistake: they give AI a task without giving it a context.

Here's the difference between a weak prompt and a strong one:

Weak Prompt

"Write me a go-to-market strategy."

Strong Prompt

"Act as a growth marketing expert who has scaled B2B SaaS from $1M to $10M ARR. I'm building a project management tool for remote teams. Write a 90-day go-to-market strategy focused on organic acquisition. Format as a week-by-week plan with specific tactics, KPIs, and success metrics."

Same AI. The second one gets you a professional-grade strategy because you gave it four things:

Role — Who the AI should be
Context — What your specific situation is
Format — How you want the output structured
Constraints — What boundaries to respect

The AI Creative Director Shift

Here's what's happening in every industry right now: AI is automating the execution layer. This week I reviewed a breakdown of someone spending $2M+ on UGC content — and they can now generate 50 variations of a video in 30 minutes that used to take a human creator 5 days.

The people getting left behind are the ones competing with AI on execution. The ones winning are the ones using AI as their execution layer while they focus on the decisions AI can't make.

Think about what this means for ALBS:

You are not competing with other accounting firms on who can crunch numbers faster. You're competing on who understands what those numbers mean for a client's business — and what decisions they should make next. AI does the crunching. You provide the judgment.

Multi-Agent Architecture: Your Digital Team

One operator I reviewed this week is running three separate income streams — with a combined operation that would normally require a 5-person team — using a multi-agent system. Here's how it works:

The Freelancing Pipeline

Agent 1: Receives client brief, parses requirements, asks clarifying questions

Agent 2: Produces deliverable using pre-loaded context files (brand voice, past work, style guides)

Agent 3: Quality review — checks for errors, formats properly

Human: 5-minute approval → delivery

What took a human writer 4 hours now takes 12 minutes of AI time. The human time is judgment and approval — not production.

For ALBS, this means: client intake → context preparation → draft → review → deliver. Each step is a specialized agent. You are the quality gate and the relationship owner.

The Weekly Board Meeting Pattern

The most powerful operational habit I found this week: run a weekly "board meeting" with your AI agents. Here's the structure:

Round 1: What was our best performing work this week? Worst? Why?

Round 2: What's happening in our market right now? What's working for others?

Round 3: Which planned work should we accelerate? Which should we kill?

Round 4: What's the blind spot? What are we not seeing?

Round 5: What's the ONE highest-leverage action for next week?

The goal isn't three priorities. It's one. Defended with numbers. Executed completely.
